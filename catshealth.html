Cats in the wild--and why they're hurting

Note: this page contains information on animal death.

Not all cats live in cozy human homes, with guaranteed cat food and scratches on the head. About 100 million of the world's cats are free-roaming, which isn't as nice as the term implies. Unlike pet cats, they are not vaccinated against disease, they face threats from larger predators, and they can produce many vulnerable kittens. Cats in the wild live only an average of 2 years while pet cats live an average of 15 years! What's driving this ridiculous statistic? Let's explore.

Types of free-roaming cats

Cats without homes have arrived there in different ways and have different levels of comfort with humans. While there is no official way to describe them, we can sort them into three categories, described by Irene Rochlitz in chapter 10 of The Domestic Cat, each with their own problems.

Disease and vaccination

Author: Senior Airman Kedesha Pennant

Free-roaming cats are vulnerable to more diseases than pet cats because they are not vaccinated. While cats rarely transmit disease to humans-- for example, there has not been a single case of cat-to-human rabies transmission in the US in the past 40 years--cats are vulnerable to diseases like this from the environment in which they live. Rabies in the US is most commonly transmitted by raccoons, bats, skunks, and foxes--all animals that wild cats are likely to encounter.

It's not just rabies, either. Living in a human home protects cats with FIV (feline immunodeficiency virus), the cat equivalent of HIV, from disease once their immune systems are weakened. And vaccines can prevent both feral cats and newly adopted cats from getting FIV. Other diseases that cats should be vaccinated for include feline distemper, feline herpesvirus, feline calicivirus, and feline leukemia virus. Without treatment, these diseases can cause health problems as wide-ranging and life-threatening as pneumonia and cancer.

Dangers for kittens

The average cat lifespan in the wild is especially driven down by the mortality rate of kittens. In general, over half of all wild kittens do not survive their first year. A study of a free-roaming cat population in Florida found that only 25% of the kittens born made it to six months of age. They were killed by disease, violence, and accidents, things that would not exist or would be greatly reduced if they lived with humans.

One female cat can have up to three litters per year, with an average of four kittens in each litter, and yet three-quarters of them may die. That's a lot of feline tragedy! Most cats should be spayed or neutered to avoid this, as well as reproductive system cancers, excessive population growth, and pregnancy complications. But living away from humans, most feral cats will never have this.

Nutter, F.B, Levine, J.F, and Stoskopf, M.K. (2004). “Reproductive capacity of free-roaming domestic cats and kitten survival rate.” Journal of the American Veterinary Medical Association, 225 (9), 1399-1402.

Abandoned cats and the struggles of strays

Stray cats--those who used to be pets--end up facing the hardest time in the wild because they didn't grow up learning to survive without humans. Only 53% of lost cats are returned to their owners as opposed to more than 80% of dogs, meaning that there are a lot of stray cats out there!

Plus, not all stray cats arrived there by being lost. Sometimes people abandon cats for a variety of reasons. We can get an idea of why people abandon cats by looking at reports from shelters:

Turner, D. & Bateson, P. (Eds.), (2013.) The Domestic Cat: The Biology of its Behaviour. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press.

As you can see, the most common reasons for people to give up cats are things that are out of their control, like allergies, or lack of education about having a cat, for example how to stop a cat from destroying household items. For these reasons, learning about proper pet care would help reduce cat abandonment and therefore improve cat welfare.
